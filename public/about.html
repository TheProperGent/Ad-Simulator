What Ad Simulator Actually Is Honesty Hour

Ad Simulator is a website where users watch advertisements and receive credits in return. I want to be very clear about what those credits are, because I believe in transparency: they are nothing. They cannot be spent. They cannot be redeemed. They do not unlock features, purchase goods, or entitle the holder to anything whatsoever. A credit is a number. The number goes up. That is the complete and exhaustive list of things a credit does.

Users watch the ads anyway. I find this enormously funny and financially very convenient. The platform generates revenue through advertising — meaning brands pay to have their ads shown, users watch those ads for free, and I collect the difference. It is, structurally, identical to every other ad-supported platform on the internet, except that I have declined to dress it up with any pretence of deeper value. This is my innovation: radical honesty about the transaction, delivered with a button that pulses invitingly.

It is, in the humblest possible assessment, the purest business model ever devised. The overhead is minimal. The product is attention. I have a great deal of respect for attention. Other people's attention, specifically.

"Credits cannot be spent. Credits cannot be redeemed. Credits are a number that goes up. That's it. That's the product." — The Founder, in response to the question "but what do the credits do?"

How It Works The Mechanics

A user signs in with their Google account and is presented with a single large button that says RUN AD. They press it. An advertisement plays. They receive one credit. The credit, as previously established, is worth nothing. This loop — press button, watch ad, number goes up — has been a cornerstone of behavioural psychology since Pavlov, and I did not invent it. I simply noticed that every app on the internet was already using it to get people to do things for free, and asked myself: could I do this, but for ads? The answer, it turns out, is yes. Emphatically yes.

You do this already. You chase likes. You maintain streaks. You check follower counts. You collect achievements in games that give you nothing. You have spent meaningful portions of your life accumulating numbers that mean nothing on platforms owned by people getting very rich from your engagement. I am simply the first person to say this out loud on the About page. You're welcome. Now please watch this ad.

The Rarity System Operant Conditioning

Each time a user watches an ad, the platform rolls the dice. Six tiers of rarity determine which ad is served. I will be honest with you about what this system is: it is operant conditioning. Variable ratio reinforcement. The same psychological mechanism that makes slot machines work, that makes loot boxes sell, that makes people spend four hours fishing in video games for a rare drop. I did not invent this mechanism. I simply applied it to advertisements and put on a nice font.

I am telling you this because nobody reads the About page. And because knowing it is a slot machine does not stop slot machines from working. That is, from a business perspective, the funniest thing I have ever encountered.

When a user discovers a new ad, the platform erupts in sparkles, colour, and fanfare. The Mythic tier reveal is, in my own words, "aggressively joyful." The user has won nothing. They have received a number. The number means nothing. They feel great about it. This is the achievement I am most proud of in my professional career.

The Collection Nothing, Beautifully Organised

Every ad a user watches is saved to their personal collection. They can accumulate duplicates, tracked with quantity badges — ×2, ×3, ×4, as high as their dedication will carry them. They can replay any ad from their collection whenever they like. They are building, in effect, a beautifully organised library of advertisements they watched in exchange for credits that do nothing. I have made this feel like an achievement. Multiple users have described the collection as "satisfying." I take this as a personal triumph.

A global leaderboard ranks users by lifetime credits, unique ads collected, and rarest finds. People are, at this very moment, competing to be number one on a leaderboard for a currency that cannot be spent. They are doing this voluntarily. Some of them are doing it enthusiastically. The internet has prepared them perfectly for this moment, and I have simply shown up to collect.

For Advertisers The Actual Point

Brands upload video or banner ads, assign them a rarity tier, and drop them into the pool. A Legendary ad is seen only by users who have watched enough ads to roll that tier — which means the audience is, by definition, maximally engaged and has demonstrated a willingness to watch advertisements in exchange for nothing. This is, from an advertiser's perspective, an extraordinarily good sign about the quality of attention being sold.

Every impression is logged in real time. Every credit earned is tracked. Every ad collected is recorded. I know exactly how many times each ad has been watched, by how many users, at what rarity tier. I have built, in short, a highly instrumented machine for turning human attention into revenue. The users are aware of this. I told them on the About page. They used the app anyway.

"You already do this for free on every other app. Here, at least one of us is getting paid." — The Founder, in his opening remarks at a conference he sponsored so he could give opening remarks

Why This Works The Uncomfortable Truth

People install ad blockers because ads are annoying. They skip ads because ads are an interruption. The entire advertising industry has spent decades trying to solve this problem by making ads louder, more targeted, more unavoidable — which has made people angrier and ad blockers more popular. I solved the problem differently: I made the ad the reason you opened the app. You are not here despite the ads. You are here for the ads. Because the ads have rarities now. Because one of them might be Mythic. Because your number might go up.

This works because the internet spent twenty years training people to find meaning in metrics. Follower counts. Likes. Achievements. Streaks. Daily login rewards. Battle pass tiers. The infrastructure of meaningless accumulation is already installed in the average internet user's brain, fully operational, and until now, entirely unmonetised from my perspective. I have simply connected it to an ad network. The users were ready. I just showed up.

Ad Simulator is free to use and always will be, because the users are not the customer. The platform is fast, reliable, and scalable to whatever audience size my ambitions require. The credits cost me nothing to produce. This is important. The credits cost me absolutely nothing to produce.
